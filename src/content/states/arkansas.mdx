officialTemplateNote: "Arkansas Department of Human Services (DHS) publishes an official fill-in Income Trust instrument — form DCO-9938, 'THE ( ) IRREVOCABLE INCOME TRUST' (revised January 2023) — authored by DHS's Division of County Operations (DCO), the office that determines Medicaid eligibility. An applicant whose gross monthly income is over the cap uses it to qualify for Nursing Facility Medicaid, the ARChoices in Homecare or DDS waivers, Living Choices assisted living, or PACE. It is an income-only, irrevocable trust that names Arkansas DHS as remainder beneficiary up to the total Medicaid paid. Arkansas calls it an 'Income Trust' (its fact sheet also calls it a 'Miller Income Trust' or 'MIT') — its version of what is generically called a Miller Trust or Qualified Income Trust; the federal authority is 42 U.S.C. § 1396p(d)(4)(B). Two Arkansas features stand out. First, funding is excess-only: the current DCO-9938 has the settlor transfer only the income that exceeds the eligibility limit (a 2023 change — the state's older PUB-396 fact sheet still describes the old 'all income' rule, and the kit flags the discrepancy). Second, the DCO-9938 directs the trustee to file an annual fiduciary tax return, so the trust is generally set up with its own EIN rather than the beneficiary's Social Security number — a departure from most states. DHS's own PUB-396 says the trust 'doesn't have to be prepared by an attorney' and offers a downloadable example, and Arkansas lets the applicant serve as their own trustee. The kit explains how the published form works and links you to DHS's own materials; unlike New Jersey and Indiana, Arkansas does not publish a separate memo to banks."

trusteeGuidanceNote: "Arkansas is unusually flexible on who serves as trustee: DHS's PUB-396 expressly allows the applicant to act as their own trustee, but advises planning for a replacement trustee in case the applicant can no longer serve — so choosing a relative, friend, or (when no relative is available) a bank as trustee or successor trustee is often the safer choice. The trust may be created by the applicant or by the applicant's child, spouse, sibling, attorney-in-fact/power of attorney, guardian, or Social-Security-appointed representative payee, and must be created on or after August 11, 1993. A trustee may serve without bond or court supervision. The trustee opens a single, separate bank account that holds only the applicant's income (kept apart from the account used for living expenses), makes only the monthly payments the caseworker authorizes on the DHS-712, delivers annual accountings to the settlor, files an annual fiduciary tax return for the trust, and on the settlor's death pays Arkansas DHS its remainder. No trustee fee may be charged for serving, and neither attorney fees nor tax-return-preparation fees may be paid from the trust — only the bank's service charges and, when a commercial institution serves as trustee, its commercially reasonable administrative fee. Confirm any complex situation with an Arkansas elder-law attorney."

uplNotes: "Arkansas regulates the unauthorized practice of law through the exclusive authority of the Arkansas Supreme Court (Ark. Const. amend. 28), backed by statutes that operate 'in aid of' that power — including Ark. Code Ann. § 16-22-206 (only those admitted by the Court may practice law) and § 16-22-211, which specifically forbids a corporation or association from furnishing legal services or advice or holding itself out as entitled to furnish legal advice. Arkansas's definition of practicing law is broad and expressly reaches trust agreements: Ark. Bar Ass'n v. Union Nat'l Bank, 224 Ark. 48, 273 S.W.2d 408 (1954), lists 'the preparation of legal instruments of all kinds' and 'writing and interpreting wills, contracts, trust agreements' as the practice of law. The controlling distinction is the fill-in-the-blanks line: under Ark. Bar Ass'n v. Block (1959) and Creekmore v. Izard (1963), merely filling in blanks as directed is not the practice of law, but eliciting a person's facts and advising or acting on them is. Arkansas has no on-point decision approving or condemning the sale of a self-help legal-forms kit — the LegalZoom UPL question in LegalZoom.com, Inc. v. McIllwain, 2013 Ark. 370, was sent to arbitration and never decided on the merits — so this posture rests on the Block/Creekmore scrivener line and the State's own self-completion framing rather than a protective holding. This kit stays on the legal-information side of that line: it does not draft, customize, or reproduce any trust instrument; it links to and explains DHS's own published DCO-9938 form, which DHS's PUB-396 states 'doesn't have to be prepared by an attorney' and for which DHS provides a downloadable example — strong evidence the State intends ordinary laypeople to complete it. The kit collects no personalized intake, gives no individualized advice, and carries a prominent 'not legal advice' disclaimer with a consent gate. Arkansas sits on the stricter end of the UPL spectrum (broad definition naming trusts, an aggressive judiciary, no forms-publishing safe harbor), so keep drafting, intake, and individualized advice at exactly zero and confirm Arkansas UPL comfort before relying on this posture."

  - question: "Who has to set up an Income Trust in Arkansas?"

  - question: "Does an Arkansas Income Trust need an EIN?"
    answer: "Arkansas is different from most states here.   - question: "How much of my income goes into the trust?"
    answer: "Only the income above the eligibility limit. The current DCO-9938 (2023) has you deposit the countable income that exceeds the cap — not all of your income. Note: DHS's older PUB-396 fact sheet still describes the pre-2023 'deposit all income' rule; follow the current form and this kit.   - question: "Can I be my own trustee?"
    answer: "Yes — Arkansas is unusual in allowing it. DHS's PUB-396 says you may act as your own trustee, but recommends naming a replacement (successor) trustee in case you can no longer serve.
