uplNotes: "Oklahoma regulates the unauthorized practice of law through the Oklahoma Supreme Court's inherent, exclusive jurisdiction (Rules Creating and Controlling the Oklahoma Bar Association, 5 O.S. Ch. 1, App. 1, Art. II), not primarily through a standalone criminal UPL statute. The controlling authority is R.J. Edwards, Inc. v. Hert, 1972 OK 151, 504 P.2d 407 (Okla. 1972) -- read in full (not merely a secondary paraphrase): municipal bond marketers who filled in bond-issuance forms prescribed by the Oklahoma Attorney General (statutorily the bond-issuers' own counsel, as ex officio Bond Commissioner under 62 O.S. §§ 11, 13) were held NOT to be practicing law, because they 'merely reproduced forms prepared by the Attorney General... and filled them out according to the directions,' acting 'as an amanuensis, secretary, or clerk for that attorney' rather than exercising a lawyer's professional judgment. This is a narrower holding than a clean Dacey-style 'forms-publishing to the general public' precedent -- it turns on an identified attorney (the AG) directing the process, with the defendant doing clerical work under that attorney's directions, not on the general public completing officially-prescribed forms with no attorney in the chain at all. The court's own governing test (adopted from ABA Model Code EC 3-5): practice of law turns on whether a service 'call[s] for the professional judgment of a lawyer' applied to another's specific facts -- and Oklahoma authorities (e.g. OBA Ethics Opinion No. 319) treat 'selecting and filling out' a legal form for a specific person as crossing that line. Net read: Oklahoma has no on-point precedent squarely blessing or condemning a generic, no-individualized-advice form/requirements-explainer sold to the public -- which is the same 'no adverse holding' posture most of our other states sit in, not a unique advantage.
