## About this book

There is a gap between two kinds of available information about Medicaid Miller Trusts. On one side, your state Medicaid agency publishes the trust template and a policy handbook section explaining the rules — accurate, authoritative, and almost completely impenetrable to anyone who hasn't already read three other Medicaid documents first. On the other side, elder-law attorneys can set up the trust for you for $1,000 to $2,500, but most families don't realize that the underlying work is mostly clerical: a one-page template, a specific kind of bank account, and a monthly routing process.

# Chapter 1 — The Medicaid income gap

There's a number every family who has ever shopped for a nursing-home bed eventually learns by heart. In Texas it's around eight thousand dollars a month. In Florida and California it can reach fifteen thousand. The national average for private-pay skilled nursing care in 2026 sits between $7,500 and $11,000 per month, depending on the state and the level of care needed. That's $90,000 to $130,000 per year. Out of pocket.

Most American families do not have that money sitting in an account waiting to be spent. The standard Social Security check in 2026 is about $1,900 a month for a retired worker. A modest pension might add another $1,500 or $2,000. Even a relatively comfortable retirement income — say $4,500 a month — covers about half of one month's nursing-home bill. After a year of paying the gap out of savings, most families have burned through whatever cushion they had.

This is the Medicaid problem. Medicare doesn't pay for long-term nursing-home care; it covers a maximum of 100 days of skilled rehab after a hospital stay, and after day 20 you pay a substantial co-insurance. Medicare Advantage doesn't help. Private long-term-care insurance, if your family member bought it twenty years ago, may help — but most Americans don't have it, and policies sold today are unaffordable for most people who didn't lock one in early.

What does cover long-term nursing-home care is **Medicaid**. Specifically, the state-administered, federally-co-funded program that pays for institutional long-term care for people who meet two tests: a *medical-necessity* test (the person actually needs nursing-facility-level care) and a *financial-eligibility* test (the person's countable income and countable assets fall below specific limits).

The financial-eligibility test is where most middle-income families get stuck. Twenty-four states — known as **income-cap states** — set a hard ceiling on the applicant's monthly countable income. In 2026, that ceiling is **$2,982 per month** for a single applicant. For a couple where both spouses are applying, it's $5,964 per month. If the applicant's income exceeds the ceiling by even one dollar, Medicaid denies coverage.

This produces what elder-law practitioners call the **income gap**: families whose loved one has too much monthly income to qualify for Medicaid, but nowhere near enough to pay $9,000 a month for a nursing-home bed. The applicant is, as the saying goes, *too rich for Medicaid and too poor for private-pay.*

The 2026 cap of $2,982/month derives from a federal calculation: 300% of the Supplemental Security Income (SSI) Federal Benefit Rate, which CMS publishes each January. In 2026 that rate is $994 per month for an individual; three times that is the $2,982 long-term-care ceiling. The cap moves each year — usually upward — as Social Security cost-of-living adjustments push the underlying SSI rate higher.

If you're reading this book, your family member is most likely in the income gap. Their countable income — Social Security, a pension, maybe a small annuity or RMD — sits above $2,982/month but well below the cost of the care they need. Without intervention, Medicaid says no, and the family writes the gap check every month out of savings until savings run out.

The intervention is a **Qualified Income Trust** — also called a **Miller Trust**. Congress created it in 1993 specifically to solve this problem. The next chapter explains what it does.

> **Verify and consult.** The 2026 figures cited above are CMS-published. Verify the current year's figures at the CMS Informational Bulletin and at your state Medicaid agency before acting. For your specific situation, consult a licensed elder-law attorney in your state.

# Chapter 2 — What a Miller Trust does

A **Miller Trust** — formally a **Qualified Income Trust (QIT)** — is an irrevocable trust authorized under federal law that lets a Medicaid applicant divert income above the state's income cap into a special bank account. Income deposited into the QIT account is **not counted** toward the income-eligibility test. The applicant remains the beneficiary of the trust; the trust's monthly distributions cover the applicant's personal expenses, any spousal support obligations, and the patient liability owed to the nursing facility.

The legal foundation is at 42 U.S.C. § 1396p(d)(4)(B). The statute defines a QIT as:

> A trust composed only of pension, Social Security, and other income to the individual (and accumulated income in the trust), if the State will receive all amounts remaining in the trust upon the death of such individual up to an amount equal to the total medical assistance paid on behalf of the individual under a State plan under this title.

Three things to notice in that sentence:

1. **"Only of pension, Social Security, and other income"** — the trust can hold only the applicant's income. It cannot hold savings, gifts from family, or any other asset. Putting non-income into a QIT account is one of the most common reasons families lose Medicaid coverage; we'll return to it in Chapter 7.

2. **"To the individual"** — the income must belong to the applicant. A spouse's separate income, a child's contribution, a relative's gift — none of these can be diverted through the QIT. Only what shows up in the applicant's own name.

3. **"The State will receive all amounts remaining in the trust upon the death of such individual"** — this is the **reversion clause** or **residuary beneficiary clause**. When the applicant dies, whatever is left in the trust account goes to the state Medicaid program, up to the total amount of Medicaid spending on the applicant's behalf. Federal law makes this mandatory. Every QIT instrument must name the state as the residual beneficiary, and every state Medicaid agency's published template includes the clause.

That's the federal frame. Twenty-four states use this mechanism; the rest don't need it because they don't have an income cap (those states allow "spend-down" or use other eligibility frameworks). Within the twenty-four income-cap states, every state's procedure looks similar but the operational details — the template language, the policy citations, the bank-account opening procedures, the trustee restrictions — vary from state to state. We'll cover the universal mechanics in this book; the state-specific operational walkthroughs are in the per-state setup kits.

## How the diversion actually works

Imagine the applicant's monthly income looks like this:

- Social Security: $2,200
- Small pension: $1,400
- Total countable income: $3,600/month

The 2026 income cap is $2,982/month. The applicant is over the cap by $618. Without a QIT, Medicaid denies the application.

With a QIT:

1. The applicant signs an irrevocable Qualified Income Trust instrument (using the state-published template). The trust names the applicant as beneficiary, names a trustee (typically an adult child or spouse), and names the state Medicaid agency as residual beneficiary up to the amount of medical assistance paid.

2. A trustee opens a bank account titled in the name of the trust. The account uses the applicant's Social Security number in most states (some states require a separate Employer Identification Number; the kit for each state confirms which).

3. Income sources are redirected so they deposit into the QIT account each month rather than into the applicant's personal account. The applicant's $2,200 Social Security check now lands in the QIT account; the $1,400 pension does too.

4. The trustee makes three distributions from the QIT account each month:
   - **Personal Needs Allowance** to the applicant — a small monthly stipend the state sets aside for the applicant's personal expenses while in the nursing facility. State amounts vary, typically $30–$75/month.
   - **Spousal allowance** (Minimum Monthly Maintenance Needs Allowance, or MMMNA) to the community spouse, if the applicant is married and the spouse remains at home or in non-institutional setting.
   - **Patient liability** (also called "share of cost") to the nursing facility — the bulk of the income, paid as the applicant's contribution to the cost of care. Medicaid then covers the difference between the patient liability and the facility's Medicaid-approved rate.

5. After the three distributions, the QIT account returns to a near-zero balance each month. The trust is not a savings account; it is a *routing structure*.

The income that passed through the QIT this way is treated as "diverted" for eligibility purposes — Medicaid does not count it. The applicant's countable income, for the income-cap test, becomes effectively zero (or whatever residual income wasn't deposited). The applicant qualifies.

## What the trust is *not*

A QIT is **not** asset planning. The applicant's countable assets — savings accounts, CDs, brokerage accounts, second properties — are governed by an entirely separate eligibility test. Most states cap countable assets at $2,000 for a single applicant (with various exemptions for the primary residence, one vehicle, prepaid funeral, and a few other categories). The QIT does nothing to address asset eligibility. If your family member has substantial countable assets, asset spend-down or asset-protection planning is a separate analysis — and one that almost always requires an attorney.

A QIT is **not** a way to leave money to your children. Whatever sits in the QIT at the applicant's death goes to the state first, up to the total Medicaid spending on the applicant's behalf. After two or three years of nursing-home care, the total Medicaid spending typically exceeds anything that could be left over in the trust. Don't think of the QIT as a savings vehicle.

A QIT is **not** a tool to hide income. Income deposited into the QIT is *disregarded for Medicaid eligibility* but is fully visible to the IRS (the account uses the applicant's SSN or trust EIN; income reports are issued normally). The QIT does not change the applicant's income tax obligation.

A QIT is **not** state-by-state different in its federal authority. The same federal statute (42 USC § 1396p(d)(4)(B)) authorizes QITs in every state that uses them. State-by-state variation is in the *operational procedure*: which template, which policy section, which bank, which trustee restrictions, which denial-letter quirks. The underlying federal mechanism is the same everywhere.

## Why "Miller Trust"?

The QIT picked up the common name "Miller Trust" from a 1990 Colorado case, *Miller v. Ibarra*, in which the court allowed an income trust to be used to qualify a Medicaid applicant whose income exceeded the state cap. Congress codified the mechanism in 1993 via the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act (OBRA-93), creating what is now 42 USC § 1396p(d)(4)(B). The "Miller Trust" name stuck even though the formal federal term is "qualified income trust."

You will see both terms used interchangeably in state agency publications, in elder-law literature, and at the bank when you go to open the account. They refer to the same instrument.

> **Verify and consult.** The federal statute is at 42 U.S.C. § 1396p(d)(4)(B). Your state Medicaid agency publishes the operational rules and the official template for your state. For your specific situation, consult a licensed elder-law attorney in your state.

# Chapter 3 — The twenty-four income-cap states

Twenty-four U.S. states use the income-cap framework that makes a Miller Trust necessary. As of January 2026, those states are:

Alabama, Alaska, Arizona, Arkansas, Colorado, Delaware, Florida, Georgia, Idaho, Indiana, Iowa, Kentucky, Mississippi, Nevada, New Jersey, New Mexico, Ohio, Oklahoma, Oregon, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, and Wyoming.

Two states use Miller Trusts conditionally:

- **Louisiana** uses an income cap but allows a Miller Trust only in certain circumstances; the analysis is more situation-dependent than in the twenty-four standard income-cap states.
- **Missouri** uses Miller Trusts specifically for Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS) waivers, not for institutional nursing-home Medicaid.

If your state isn't on the list, your family member may still be eligible for Medicaid long-term care without a Miller Trust. The non-income-cap states use a "medically needy" or "spend-down" framework: instead of failing for being over an income cap, the applicant qualifies by demonstrating that their medical expenses consume the excess income each month. The mechanics are different, the paperwork is different, and a Miller Trust is not the right tool. If you live in a non-income-cap state, a brief conversation with an elder-law attorney about your state's spend-down rules is the next step.

# Chapter 4 — Do you need one? A decision walkthrough

Not every family above the income cap needs a Miller Trust. Walk through this short checklist before assuming you do.

## Step 1: Does your state use an income cap?

If your state is on the list in Chapter 3, yes. If it isn't, your state uses a different eligibility framework (typically "medically needy" or "spend-down"), and a Miller Trust is not the right tool. Stop here and consult an elder-law attorney in your state about the spend-down framework.

## Step 2: What is the applicant's countable monthly income?

Add up every recurring source of income the applicant receives that **counts** for Medicaid eligibility purposes. Common countable income sources:

- Social Security retirement benefits
- Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI)
- Railroad Retirement Board benefits
- Pensions (private employer or government)
- Annuity payments
- Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) from retirement accounts
- VA pension and compensation
- Any other recurring monthly payment to the applicant

A small number of income sources are **not** countable for Medicaid eligibility. The most common exclusion is **VA aid and attendance benefits**, **housebound allowances**, and **reimbursements for unusual or continuing medical expenses** — these are exempt from both the income-eligibility test and the patient-liability calculation in most states. If the applicant receives a VA pension that includes these components, separate them out before counting countable income. (Aid and attendance benefits should also *not* be deposited into the QIT once you set one up — but more on that in the operational kits.)

Use the gross amount of each source, before any deductions (before Medicare premiums, before tax withholding, before voluntary deductions like life insurance premiums).

## Step 3: Compare to the cap

The 2026 single-applicant cap is **$2,982/month**. The couple cap (both spouses applying) is **$5,964/month**. If countable income is at or below the cap, you do not need a QIT — proceed directly to the Medicaid application with confidence about the income side of eligibility.

If countable income is above the cap, even by one dollar, the Medicaid application will be denied on income grounds unless a QIT is set up first.

## Step 4: Confirm the applicant is over the income cap *and* needs nursing-facility-level care

The income cap matters only for Medicaid long-term-care benefits — nursing-home Medicaid, Home and Community-Based Services waivers, and a few related categories. A Miller Trust does not help with regular community Medicaid, Medicare Savings Programs, or other non-LTC benefit programs.

Confirm three things before proceeding:

1. The applicant needs nursing-facility-level care (or HCBS waiver services) — a doctor's assessment will be part of the eventual Medicaid application.
2. The applicant is over the income cap.
3. The applicant otherwise meets Medicaid eligibility — citizenship, residency in the state, and the countable-resource test (generally $2,000 for a single applicant, with several exempt categories).

If any of those three is uncertain, a brief attorney consultation is worth the cost. Misjudging any of them can mean months of denied coverage and tens of thousands of dollars of private-pay liability.

## Step 5: Decide whether to DIY or hire an attorney

If your situation is what we'll call "standard" — the applicant is over the cap by a modest amount (a few hundred to a few thousand dollars per month), the income sources are routine (Social Security plus pension, plus maybe a small annuity), the applicant's assets are at or below the resource limit, and there are no complications like prior gifting or a separately-titled second home — then a Miller Trust setup is well within DIY territory. The state-specific Miller Trust Setup Kits at millertrustguide.com walk through the operational steps state by state.

If any of the following apply, **call an attorney**:

- The applicant has trusts other than the QIT (revocable living trust, special needs trust, irrevocable Medicaid asset-protection trust, etc.)
- The applicant has transferred assets (gifts to children, transfers to family members for less than fair value) in the last five years
- The applicant has countable assets significantly above $2,000 (asset spend-down or restructuring is its own discipline)
- The applicant is the community spouse rather than the institutionalized spouse, and you're uncertain about the Community Spouse Resource Allowance, MMMNA, or other community-spouse protections
- The applicant owns real estate other than the primary residence
- The applicant has a complicated income picture (multiple pensions, business income, rental income, foreign income)
- The Medicaid application has already been denied for a substantive reason
- You suspect fraud, identity theft, or any criminal-history concern

The Chapter 9 list expands on each of these. The short version: a Miller Trust handles one specific eligibility problem cleanly. Layered situations with multiple moving parts benefit from a professional eye, and the cost of an attorney consultation ($300–500 for a one-time review) is much smaller than the cost of a denial that delays Medicaid coverage by months.

> **Verify and consult.** This walkthrough is a triage tool, not a substitute for legal advice. For your specific situation, consult a licensed elder-law attorney in your state.

# Chapter 5 — Who can serve as trustee

The **trustee** is the person who manages the QIT bank account each month. They deposit the applicant's income (or confirm direct-deposit redirects landed correctly), they make the three required distributions (personal needs allowance, spousal allowance if applicable, patient liability to the nursing facility), and they keep the records that prove the trust ran correctly.

Trustee work is **modest but unrelenting**. The actual time commitment after the account is open is one to two hours per month — confirming deposits, writing or transferring distributions, filing the bank statement. The unrelenting part is that it has to happen *every month, on time*. A missed month of distributions is a denied month of Medicaid coverage, and worse, can be treated by the state as a transfer of assets that triggers a penalty period of ineligibility (more on that in Chapter 6).

## Who can serve

Federal law is silent on who can serve as trustee of a QIT. State Medicaid agencies make recommendations rather than hard rules in most cases. The most common choices, in order of frequency:

1. **An adult child of the applicant**, especially one who already holds Power of Attorney for the applicant's finances. This is the most common arrangement we see. The adult child is geographically near the applicant, has access to the applicant's financial documents, and has standing motivation to keep the trust running smoothly.

2. **The community spouse** of the applicant, when the applicant is the institutionalized spouse and the community spouse remains at home or in a non-institutional setting. The community spouse is often retired, lives near the bank, and has personal stake in the trust working correctly.

3. **A friend or other family member** with the time, judgment, and proximity to manage the monthly work — typically a sibling of the applicant or a longtime family friend with Power of Attorney.

4. **A professional trustee**, such as a bank trust department or a fee-based fiduciary. This is rare for QITs because the monthly fees ($50–200/month) consume a noticeable share of the applicant's income, but it is occasionally the right choice when no family member is willing or able to serve.

## Who probably shouldn't serve

State Medicaid agencies typically recommend against the **applicant themselves** serving as trustee. The reasoning is straightforward: the applicant is the beneficiary, and if the applicant fails to comply with the trust's distribution requirements (because of cognitive decline, hospitalization, or any other reason), the applicant loses Medicaid eligibility. Naming a different person as trustee builds a layer of separation between the beneficiary's situation and the trust's operations.

In some states, additional restrictions apply. The institutional facility providing care to the applicant is often discouraged or prohibited from serving as trustee, because the facility is the recipient of the trust's largest monthly distribution (the patient liability) and the structural conflict of interest is obvious. Check your state's specific guidance — your state's Miller Trust Setup Kit walks through state-specific trustee guidance.

## What the trustee actually does each month

The monthly rhythm has three parts.

**Part 1: Confirm income deposited.** Once the direct-deposit redirects are in place (more on this in Chapter 6), the applicant's listed income sources land in the QIT bank account each month. The trustee logs in to the bank's online portal (or reviews the paper statement) and confirms that every expected deposit arrived. If a deposit is missing, the trustee chases it down with the income source — Social Security, the pension administrator, the VA — before the end of the month.

**Part 2: Make the three distributions.**

- Distribute the Personal Needs Allowance to the applicant. State amounts vary; common ranges are $30 to $75 per month. This is usually a transfer to the applicant's personal account or a check the applicant can use for personal expenses at the facility.
- Distribute the spousal allowance to the community spouse, if applicable. The amount is determined by the state's MMMNA calculation, which is itself state-specific.
- Distribute the patient liability (also called "share of cost") to the nursing facility. The facility issues an invoice each month; the patient-liability amount is on the invoice.

**Part 3: File the records.**

The trustee should keep a monthly file containing:

- The bank statement showing the month's deposits and distributions
- A copy of the check (or transfer confirmation) for each of the three distributions
- The facility invoice that documents the patient-liability amount

These records are the trustee's defense if the state Medicaid agency ever audits the trust (which happens occasionally during periodic eligibility reviews) and they are also the trustee's defense against any later question of fiduciary breach.

## Fiduciary duties in plain English

When you accept the role of trustee, you accept a **fiduciary duty** to manage the trust in the beneficiary's best interest. In practice this means:

- Don't commingle trust funds with your own money. The QIT account is the trust's; your personal accounts are yours.
- Don't use trust funds for any purpose other than the required distributions. You can't borrow from the QIT, you can't pay your own expenses from it, and you can't redirect distributions to anyone other than the authorized recipients.
- Don't delay distributions. The federal-statute language and state policy both treat delay as a problem; state agencies in particular treat trustee distribution delays as transfers of assets.
- Keep records. The trustee is the only person who knows what happened in the trust month to month. Those records are your defense if anything is questioned later.

If serving as trustee feels like a heavier responsibility than you expected, that's appropriate — it is one. But it's also a manageable monthly task once the systems are set up. The state-specific setup kits walk through the system setup state by state.

> **Verify and consult.** Trustee duties are governed by both federal trust law and your state's trust statutes. For your specific situation, consult a licensed attorney in your state.

# Chapter 6 — The basic mechanics — funding, distribution, the monthly rhythm

The actual mechanics of running a QIT have three phases: **setup** (do once), **the first month** (the most error-prone), and **steady-state monthly operation** (manageable).

## Setup — sign, open, redirect

A valid QIT setup requires three things to happen in the **same calendar month**:

1. The trust instrument is **signed and dated** by the applicant (or by the trustee under a valid Power of Attorney for the applicant).

2. A **QIT bank account is opened** in the name of the trust. The account is typically opened with the applicant's Social Security number (some states require a separate EIN; check your state's specific requirement).

3. **Income is deposited** into the QIT account in an amount sufficient to bring the applicant's remaining countable income below the state's income cap.

Medicaid eligibility begins the calendar month all three are true. If any of the three slips into the next month, eligibility starts the following month. This is the fundamental timing mechanic of a QIT and missing it is the most common reason for delayed coverage.

There is one important nuance: **retroactive eligibility**. If a qualifying QIT was set up and operating in months *before* the Medicaid application was filed, the income disregard can be applied up to **three months retroactive** to the application file date, provided all other eligibility requirements were also met in those months. This rarely comes up in practice (most families don't set up a QIT before applying), but it's worth knowing about — if your family member's QIT has been operating for a month or two before you submit the Medicaid application, those prior months may still qualify.

## The first month — partial deposit is OK

The first month of a QIT is usually messy. Direct-deposit redirects take time to process — Social Security typically takes 1–2 business days for online changes and up to several weeks for mailed forms; pensions take a full pay cycle; the VA takes about as long as Social Security. Almost no family successfully redirects everything in time for the first month.

State Medicaid agencies recognize this and accommodate it. **A partial deposit in the initial month is allowed** and does not invalidate the QIT. If only part of an income source made it into the QIT in the first month — for example, the applicant paid some bills before the trust was established and only the remainder landed in the QIT — the QIT is still valid and the entire amount of that income source is disregarded for that month.

This grace applies *only* to the initial month. In every month after the initial month, **the entire listed source must be deposited**. Partial diversion of a listed source in any non-initial month invalidates the QIT for that month and triggers an income-ineligible determination.

Most families bridge the first-month gap by manually transferring the missing portion of income into the QIT account from the applicant's personal account, in the same calendar month the trust is signed. Document the manual transfer with a memo line that identifies it as the QIT funding deposit for that month. The state-specific setup kits cover this in operational detail.

## Steady-state — the monthly rhythm

Once the redirects are in place and the first month is behind you, the monthly rhythm becomes routine:

- **Income arrives** in the QIT account via direct deposit (Social Security on its assigned day; the pension on its day; the VA on its day).
- **Trustee logs in to the bank** and confirms deposits.
- **Trustee makes the three distributions** — Personal Needs Allowance to the applicant, spousal allowance to the community spouse if applicable, patient liability to the nursing facility.
- **Account returns to a near-zero balance.**
- **Trustee files the records** for that month.

The next month, it happens again.

## The distribution deadline

This is the single most important operational rule and the one most likely to bite a trustee who isn't watching carefully:

> **Income deposited into the QIT account must be distributed by the last day of the *following* month.**

If income is deposited in March, the trustee has until April 30 to make the required distributions. **Late distribution is treated as a transfer of assets**, which can trigger a Medicaid penalty period — a period of weeks or months during which the applicant is ineligible for benefits even though they otherwise qualify.

Set a recurring monthly reminder. Make distributions on the same day each month, well before the deadline. Don't wait until the last week of the following month — anything that prevents you from completing distributions on time (you're sick, you travel, the bank's wire system is down) becomes a Medicaid problem.

## What goes wrong with the monthly rhythm

Three categories of problems crop up.

**Missed deposits.** The redirect didn't process and the income landed in the applicant's personal account instead of the QIT. The income counts as the applicant's for that month; the applicant becomes income-ineligible for that month. You cannot retroactively cure by transferring the money later — the deposit had to have *occurred* in the calendar month the income was received. Resume normal funding the following month; eligibility resumes the following month; the lost month is billed at private-pay rates.

**Wrong-source deposits.** Income from a source *not* listed in the trust shows up in the QIT account. This is less catastrophic — the deposit doesn't necessarily invalidate the trust — but it requires correction. If the source's full monthly amount stays under the income cap when added to other countable income, eligibility may not be affected. If it pushes the applicant over the cap, the month is at risk.

**Resource deposits.** A family member transfers money into the QIT account thinking they're being helpful. A bank deposits an interest payment. The applicant accidentally cashes a refund check into the QIT account. **The QIT account is income only**. Any resource deposited into the account risks turning the entire account balance into a countable resource for Medicaid purposes, which can push the applicant over the $2,000 resource limit and trigger an ineligibility determination.

This last problem is the most dangerous because it's the easiest to do by accident and the hardest to undo. Family members need to know, in plain terms: *no deposits to the trust account from anyone but the applicant's listed income sources. Ever.*

The state-specific setup kits include monthly checklists that catch each of these problems before they become Medicaid issues. For now, the key thing to know is that the monthly rhythm is manageable but requires discipline.

> **Verify and consult.** The federal-statute basis is at 42 U.S.C. § 1396p(d)(4)(B). Your state's specific deposit and distribution mechanics are in your state Medicaid agency's policy handbook. For your specific situation, consult a licensed elder-law attorney in your state.

# Chapter 7 — Three things that commonly go wrong

State Medicaid agencies publish denial statistics for QITs (in varying levels of detail). The patterns are remarkably consistent across states. Three categories of error account for the large majority of denials.

This chapter covers the three categories at the level of *what* goes wrong. The state-specific *why* — the precise policy section number behind each denial cause, the exact language the agency uses on the denial letter, the appeal procedure — is the subject of the state-specific kits, where each state's complete denial-avoidance checklist lives.

## Category 1 — Timing failures

The most common QIT denial is a timing failure. Four flavors of this:

**1a — The trust was signed in one calendar month and funded in the next.** Medicaid eligibility for that first month is denied. The QIT only achieves the income disregard for a month if it was signed AND funded in that same calendar month. A trust signed on the 30th of March and funded on April 2nd will not qualify for March — coverage starts in April.

**1b — Income was deposited into the applicant's personal account instead of the QIT account.** This usually happens when a direct-deposit redirect takes longer than expected. In the initial month, a partial deposit is acceptable; in any later month, the *full* source amount must land in the QIT. If the entire month's income from a listed source missed the QIT, the source's full amount is countable for the applicant that month and the income-cap test fails.

**1c — The trustee distributed late.** Income deposited in March must be distributed by April 30 — the last day of the *following* month. Distributions that slip past that deadline are treated as transfers of assets, which can trigger a Medicaid penalty period of weeks to months of ineligibility.

**1d — The Medicaid application was filed before the QIT was funded.** This is a misordering problem. The QIT must be operating before (or, at the latest, in the same month as) the month for which Medicaid coverage is sought. Filing the Medicaid application first and then setting up the QIT can mean the early months of the application period are evaluated against pre-QIT income — and fail.

The fix in every case is the same: **plan the month carefully**. Sign early in a month, fund the trust the same day or within a few days, then file the Medicaid application. If income redirects haven't fully processed, manually transfer the first month's income from the applicant's personal account into the QIT account in that same calendar month.

## Category 2 — Trust-instrument defects

The QIT must comply with the federal statute (42 USC § 1396p(d)(4)(B)) AND with the state Medicaid agency's specific template requirements. Both. Defects in either layer cause denials.

**2a — The trust is not properly irrevocable.** Federal law requires the QIT to be irrevocable. Per CMS guidance, a trust that says it is irrevocable but allows revocation through court action does not meet the irrevocability requirement. State agencies typically reject any QIT instrument that contains a court-action revocation clause. The fix: use the state's published template, which has the right irrevocability language already drafted.

**2b — The reversion clause is missing or weak.** Federal law requires the QIT to name the state Medicaid agency as the residuary beneficiary up to the total amount of medical assistance paid. The state-published template has this clause pre-drafted; *do not edit it, weaken it, or remove it.* A defective reversion clause is one of the easier denials to incur — and one of the most preventable.

**2c — Income sources are not listed correctly.** The trust must enumerate the income sources whose payments will be deposited into the QIT account. A source that pays the applicant but isn't listed in the trust cannot be deposited into the QIT without amending the trust. List every recurring income source the applicant receives that counts toward Medicaid eligibility before signing the trust.

**2d — Use of a non-state-published template.** Many states explicitly require their own template. Trusts drafted from generic legal-forms websites, attorney boilerplate, or "Medicaid trust template" downloads from non-state sources are commonly rejected — sometimes on substantive grounds (the template doesn't include a state-required clause) and sometimes on procedural grounds (the state Medicaid agency only reviews trusts on its own published template). When in doubt, download the template from your state Medicaid agency's official .gov website on the day you sign.

## Category 3 — Operational drift

Even a QIT that is set up correctly can drift into denial territory over months and years of operation. Three patterns:

**3a — Resources deposited into the QIT account.** The QIT is income-only. A well-meaning family member who transfers savings into the account, a bank that credits a refund or interest payment, or an applicant who accidentally cashes a non-income check into the account can convert the entire QIT balance into a countable resource — which, if it exceeds $2,000, makes the applicant resource-ineligible. The fix is preventive: the trustee must educate everyone in the family that the QIT account receives only the listed income sources, ever.

**3b — Trustee fails to distribute the required amounts.** Whether by oversight, illness, or misunderstanding, a month goes by without the Personal Needs Allowance distributed, the spousal allowance distributed, or the patient-liability check sent to the facility. The undistributed income, depending on how long it sits, can be treated as a transfer of assets or as accumulated resource. Both create Medicaid problems.

**3c — A listed source's payment changes in amount or schedule and the trust isn't updated.** Social Security cost-of-living adjustments increase the applicant's monthly Social Security check each January. If the QIT's funding worksheet was prepared at the old amount and the trustee continues to distribute at the old amount, the patient-liability calculation is off — sometimes leading to underpayment to the facility, sometimes to retained funds in the trust account that build into a countable resource over months. Refresh the worksheet each January.

The above is the **what**. The state-specific *how to avoid each one* lives in the per-state setup kits — including, for your state:

- The exact agency-published list of denial reasons for your state

- The annual update checklist to run each January after the CMS Federal Benefit Rate adjusts

The book covers the universal categories. The kit covers your state's complete operational picture.

# Chapter 8 — What happens when the applicant dies

The Qualified Income Trust does not survive the applicant. When the beneficiary dies, the trust's federal statutory purpose — diverting income to qualify a living applicant for Medicaid — is complete. What remains is a wind-down process the trustee handles.

## The reversion clause activates

The QIT's reversion clause says the state Medicaid agency receives the remaining trust balance up to the **total amount of medical assistance** paid on the applicant's behalf during their lifetime. This is the trade for using a QIT in the first place: the federal mechanism allows income diversion for eligibility purposes on the condition that the state recovers from the trust at death.

In practice, the wind-down looks like this.

## Trustee duties on death

**Step 1: Notify the state Medicaid agency.** State requirements vary; typically a written notice within 10 to 30 days of death is required. The agency will send a final accounting request.

**Step 2: Stop further deposits.** Contact each income source — Social Security, pension administrator, VA — and stop direct deposits to the QIT. Social Security in particular will request return of any payments issued for periods after the date of death (Social Security pays in arrears, so the check that arrives on the third of the month is for the previous month — but any payment received for a month *after* death must be returned).

**Step 3: Make a final partial-month distribution.** For the month of death, the trustee distributes the Personal Needs Allowance, the spousal allowance, and the patient-liability share according to the trust's distribution rules, typically prorated for the days of the month the applicant was alive.

**Step 4: Wait for the state's final accounting.** Do not distribute any remaining balance until you have the state's accounting in writing. The accounting shows the total Medicaid spending on the applicant's behalf — the cap on what the state recovers from the trust.

**Step 5: Pay the state.** State-specific instructions vary. Some states require cashier's check or money order; some accept personal check. Some require a specific receipt form for the payment. Your state's setup kit covers the specific payment mechanics; in general, the trustee writes a check to the state Medicaid agency for the lesser of the remaining trust balance or the total Medicaid spending.

**Step 6: Distribute any remainder to the secondary residual beneficiary.** Most QIT templates have a secondary residual clause: after the state is paid, anything remaining passes to a named party (often the applicant's children, the applicant's probate estate, or a charity). Anything that's left after the state gets its share goes to that secondary beneficiary.

**Step 7: Close the QIT account.** Once all distributions are complete and the state has confirmed the trust's obligations are satisfied, close the bank account.

## On estate recovery generally

The QIT reversion clause is **one piece** of the larger Medicaid estate-recovery framework. Every state has the right, under federal law, to recover Medicaid spending from the applicant's estate after death. The estate includes the applicant's assets owned at death — typically the home (if still owned), any vehicles, any remaining savings or investments, and the residual interest in the QIT.

Estate-recovery rules are **fact-intensive and state-specific**. Common considerations:

- **Homestead protections** — some states protect the primary residence from estate recovery if a surviving spouse, dependent child, or other qualifying occupant remains in the home.
- **Surviving-spouse exemptions** — most states defer estate recovery while a surviving spouse is alive; recovery happens against the spouse's estate after the spouse's death (though some states have abolished this and recover directly from the applicant's estate at death).
- **Dependent-relative exemptions** — some states protect the estate when a disabled or minor child of the applicant survives.
- **Hardship waivers** — states have procedures to waive or reduce recovery in cases of family hardship.

This book does not address estate recovery beyond the QIT's own reversion clause. **If the applicant owns a home, has assets outside the QIT, or has a surviving spouse with potential estate interests, consult a licensed elder-law attorney in your state for the estate-recovery analysis.** Decisions made in advance of the applicant's death — about home ownership, beneficiary designations on accounts, jointly-titled assets — can dramatically change the post-death estate-recovery picture. An hour with an attorney on this question, before the death, can save tens of thousands of dollars later.

> **Verify and consult.** Estate recovery is state-by-state. Your state Medicaid agency publishes the post-death recovery procedure. For your specific situation, especially anything beyond the QIT itself, consult a licensed elder-law attorney in your state.

# Chapter 9 — When to call an attorney — and how to find one

This chapter is the most important practical chapter in the book. The Miller Trust setup itself is, for many families, do-it-yourself territory. But "many families" is not "all families," and the situations that benefit from professional help are common enough that most readers of this book will benefit from at least an initial consultation.

## Eight situations that call for an attorney

**1. The applicant has trusts other than the QIT.**

Revocable living trusts, special needs trusts, irrevocable Medicaid asset-protection trusts, supplemental needs trusts — these interact with the QIT analysis in ways that vary by state and by trust language. The wrong move can disqualify the applicant from Medicaid or trigger a transfer-penalty period. An attorney who reviews the existing trusts can often resolve the question in a single consultation.

**2. The applicant has made gifts or transferred assets in the last five years.**

Federal Medicaid law imposes a 60-month (5-year) "look-back period" before the Medicaid application date. Uncompensated transfers of assets during the look-back can trigger a transfer penalty — a period of ineligibility for Medicaid benefits that runs after eligibility would otherwise begin. The math is state-specific and the analysis often requires reconstructing five years of financial history. This is not DIY territory.

**3. The applicant has countable assets significantly above $2,000.**

Asset spend-down planning is its own discipline. Buying exempt assets (a more comfortable primary residence, prepaid funeral arrangements, a newer car), purchasing certain types of annuities, restructuring jointly-titled accounts, or transferring a home to a caregiver child — each has rules and risks. The asset-side analysis is separate from the QIT and almost always benefits from professional structuring.

**4. The applicant is the community spouse rather than the institutionalized spouse.**

If the applicant is the spouse who stays at home while the other spouse enters nursing care, the eligibility analysis runs through the **Community Spouse Resource Allowance (CSRA)** and the **Minimum Monthly Maintenance Needs Allowance (MMMNA)**. Both have state-specific calculations and both can dramatically affect what the at-home spouse keeps. An attorney consultation on the community-spouse side often pays for itself in the first month of optimized benefits.

**5. The applicant owns a home (homestead) and there is no surviving spouse, dependent child, or other exemption-qualifying occupant.**

Homestead disposition during Medicaid eligibility and post-death estate recovery is fact-specific. Decisions made about the home — keeping it, transferring it, renting it out, selling it before applying — have implications that ripple into eligibility, tax, and estate-recovery analysis. Attorney input here is high-leverage.

**6. The applicant has a complicated income picture.**

Multiple pensions from different employers (or different countries), rental income, business income, irregular trust distributions, royalty income, partnership distributions — any of these complicate the income-side analysis. The decision about which income sources to list in the QIT, and how to handle income that doesn't fit cleanly into the worksheet, may benefit from professional review.

**7. The Medicaid application has already been denied for a substantive reason.**

If the application was denied for a *fixable drafting error* (wrong template, missing reversion clause, missed signing month), a corrected refiling usually solves it. If the application was denied for a *substantive reason* (transfer penalty, over-resource determination, denied medical necessity), an appeal is the next step. Appeals have statutory deadlines and require legal posture. Don't appeal without an attorney.

**8. You suspect fraud, identity theft, or any criminal-history concern.**

This is outside the scope of any informational book or operational kit. If there's any indication of fraud — whether by the applicant, by a family member, or against the applicant — get an attorney immediately.

## How to find a licensed elder-law attorney

Three reliable starting points:

**Your state bar's lawyer referral service.** Every state bar association maintains a lawyer referral and information service. The service matches you with a bar-member attorney whose practice includes elder law or Medicaid planning. Most bar referrals offer a low-cost initial consultation (commonly $30 for 30 minutes) — enough time to scope your situation and decide whether full representation is needed or whether one-time advice will resolve it.

Search for "[your state] bar lawyer referral service" and look for the official state bar website.

**National Academy of Elder Law Attorneys (NAELA).** NAELA members specialize in elder law as a practice area. The NAELA find-a-lawyer directory is at naela.org/findlawyer. NAELA membership is voluntary; it signals practice focus rather than credential, but membership requires CLE credits in elder law and is meaningful.

**National Elder Law Foundation (NELF).** NELF certifies **Certified Elder Law Attorneys (CELAs)** through a process the American Bar Association accredits. CELA is the recognized specialty credential for elder-law practice in the United States. Most states have a small number of CELAs; the NELF directory is at nelf.org/attorney-directory-search.

**For low-income families**, additional resources include:

- Local **legal aid offices** (state legal aid offices provide free or low-cost legal services to low-income individuals)
- Local **area agency on aging** (federally-designated agencies that coordinate elder services in every region; many maintain lists of elder-law attorneys who accept reduced-fee consultations)
- The local **bar association** in your county or city, in addition to the state bar
- **Disability Rights organizations** in your state, particularly if disability is a factor in the eligibility picture

## What to bring to the consultation

If you walk into an attorney's office having read this book and prepared the following, you'll get more from a 30-minute consultation than most people get from an hour:

1. **A list of every income source the applicant receives**, with the monthly gross amount for each (Social Security, pensions, annuities, RMDs, VA benefits, other).
2. **A list of every asset the applicant owns**, with rough current value (savings accounts, brokerage accounts, vehicles, home, life insurance with cash value, business interests).
3. **A list of every uncompensated transfer the applicant has made in the last five years** (gifts to children, payments to family for less than fair value, charitable transfers, asset retitling).
4. **The names and contact information for any existing trusts, powers of attorney, advance directives, or estate-planning documents** the applicant has.
5. **A short written description of the applicant's medical situation and current care setting**, including the date of any nursing-facility admission and the facility name.
6. **Your specific questions**, written out in advance.

A prepared client lets the attorney spend the consultation on judgment and analysis rather than on intake. Most attorneys notice and appreciate it.

> **Verify and consult.** This chapter's list is a starting framework. Your situation may have additional dimensions that aren't covered here. For your specific situation, consult a licensed elder-law attorney in your state.

# Back matter

## Glossary

**Applicant** — The person applying for Medicaid long-term-care benefits.

**Beneficiary** — In a Miller Trust, the beneficiary is the Medicaid applicant. The trust holds and distributes the beneficiary's income on the beneficiary's behalf.

**CELA** — Certified Elder Law Attorney. The recognized specialty credential for elder-law practice in the United States, certified by the National Elder Law Foundation (NELF) through an ABA-accredited process.

**CMS** — Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services. The federal agency that oversees Medicaid policy and publishes the annual Federal Benefit Rate that drives the income-cap calculation.

**Community spouse** — The non-applicant spouse of a married Medicaid applicant. The community spouse typically remains in non-institutional setting while the applicant receives nursing-facility care.

**Countable income** — Income that counts toward the Medicaid income-eligibility test. Most income is countable; specific exclusions include VA aid and attendance benefits and unusual-medical-expense reimbursements.

**Countable resources (or countable assets)** — Assets that count toward the Medicaid resource-eligibility test. Most states cap countable resources at $2,000 for a single applicant. Common exemptions include the primary residence, one vehicle, prepaid funeral, and certain personal effects.

**Distribution** — A payment from the QIT account to one of the authorized recipients (the applicant for the Personal Needs Allowance, the community spouse for the MMMNA, the nursing facility for the patient liability).

**EIN** — Employer Identification Number. A federal tax ID separate from a Social Security number. Some states require the QIT account to have its own EIN; most allow the account to use the applicant's SSN.

**Estate recovery** — A state's right under federal law to seek recovery of Medicaid spending from the applicant's estate after death.

**Federal Benefit Rate (FBR)** — The monthly Supplemental Security Income (SSI) benefit amount, published by CMS each January. In 2026, the FBR is $994 for an individual. Three times the FBR is the standard income-cap-state ceiling for long-term-care Medicaid eligibility.

**Fiduciary duty** — The legal obligation of a trustee (or other fiduciary) to act in the best interest of the beneficiary, with care, loyalty, and good faith.

**HCBS** — Home and Community-Based Services. Medicaid programs that pay for long-term-care services delivered in the applicant's home or community rather than in a nursing facility. HCBS programs have the same income-cap eligibility test as institutional Medicaid in income-cap states.

**Income-cap state** — A state that uses a hard monthly income ceiling for Medicaid long-term-care eligibility (currently 24 states). Applicants above the cap must use a Miller Trust to qualify.

**Look-back period** — The 60-month (5-year) window before the Medicaid application date during which uncompensated asset transfers may trigger a transfer penalty.

**Medicaid for the Elderly and People with Disabilities (MEPD)** — The state-administered Medicaid program category that covers long-term-care eligibility for elderly and disabled adults.

**Medicaid long-term care** — Medicaid coverage for nursing-facility care, intermediate-care facility services, and HCBS waiver services. Distinct from regular community Medicaid coverage.

**Miller Trust** — Common name for a Qualified Income Trust. See "QIT."

**MMMNA** — Minimum Monthly Maintenance Needs Allowance. The minimum monthly income the community spouse is allowed to keep when the institutionalized spouse qualifies for Medicaid. State-specific calculation.

**NAELA** — National Academy of Elder Law Attorneys. The membership organization for attorneys whose practice includes elder law. naela.org/findlawyer.

**NELF** — National Elder Law Foundation. The credentialing body that certifies CELAs. nelf.org.

**OBRA-93** — The Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1993. The federal legislation that codified the Qualified Income Trust mechanism and the broader Medicaid eligibility framework currently in use.

**Patient liability** (also "share of cost") — The portion of the applicant's monthly income that the trustee pays to the nursing facility as the applicant's contribution to the cost of care. Medicaid pays the difference between the patient liability and the facility's Medicaid-approved rate.

**Personal Needs Allowance (PNA)** — The amount of the applicant's income retained each month for personal expenses while in a nursing facility. State-specific; commonly $30–$75/month.

**Power of Attorney (POA)** — A legal document by which one person (the principal) authorizes another (the agent) to act on their behalf. Many QIT trustees serve under POA for the applicant.

**Qualified Income Trust (QIT)** — An irrevocable trust authorized under 42 USC § 1396p(d)(4)(B) that allows a Medicaid applicant to divert income above the state's income cap, qualifying for long-term-care benefits. Same instrument as a Miller Trust.

**Residuary beneficiary (or reversion clause)** — The clause in the QIT instrument that names the recipient of the trust's remaining balance at the applicant's death. Federal law requires the state Medicaid agency to be named as the residual beneficiary up to the total amount of medical assistance paid.

**SSI** — Supplemental Security Income. The federal income-support program for low-income elderly, blind, and disabled people. The SSI Federal Benefit Rate is the basis for the Medicaid long-term-care income cap.

**Special income limit** — The 300%-of-FBR ceiling used in income-cap states for long-term-care Medicaid eligibility. $2,982/month for a single applicant in 2026.

**Spend-down state** — A state that does not use an income cap but instead allows the applicant to qualify by demonstrating that medical expenses consume the excess income each month. The non-Miller-Trust framework used in roughly half the states.

**Transfer of assets** — An uncompensated transfer of the applicant's assets to another party, which (during the look-back period) can trigger a Medicaid transfer penalty.

**Transfer penalty** — A period of Medicaid ineligibility imposed when the applicant has made uncompensated transfers during the look-back period. Penalty length depends on the value transferred and the state's monthly penalty divisor.

**Trustee** — The person who manages the QIT bank account and makes the monthly distributions. Typically an adult child of the applicant, the community spouse, or another trusted family member with Power of Attorney.

## About the Miller Trust Setup Kit

This book introduces the Miller Trust mechanism. The state-specific setup kits walk through the operational steps for a particular state.

Each setup kit includes:

- **The bank-account walkthrough**: the verbatim opening script for the branch, the five most common refusals with the response to each, and a printable resolution letter for the branch manager. This is the section most buyers tell other buyers about.
- **The complete state-specific denial-avoidance checklist** with the precise policy citation behind each common denial cause.
- **The monthly funding worksheet**, pre-filled with the current year's income cap and Personal Needs Allowance.
- **The direct link to the state Medicaid agency's official QIT template** and a plain-English walkthrough of every field you fill in yourself.
- **The 'what to say to family members' page** — a short script for when a sibling asks why you didn't just hire an attorney.
- **The month-by-month income redirect process** for Social Security, pension, VA benefits, and annuity sources.
- **The state agency citations index** — every operational claim in the kit cites a primary state agency document.
